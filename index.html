Artifact 01 — Workshop 1 — Assignment 1.4

Digital Safety AI — A Collaborative Cybersecurity Awareness Chatbot

AI Lab

As part of a collaborative group lab with 8 team members, I explored generative AI tools and applied the Design Thinking framework to co-build Digital Safety AI — a cybersecurity awareness chatbot built as a Custom GPT. This artifact demonstrates practical AI development skills, ethical design thinking, and the ability to translate a real user problem into a working AI solution.

Objective

To gain hands-on experience with generative AI technologies, understand how prompting quality affects output, and apply the Design Thinking framework collaboratively to build an AI assistant that educates users on cybersecurity basics and safe online behavior.

Description

Digital Safety AI educates users on common internet threats, phishing identification, password security, safe Wi-Fi usage, and responsible online behavior — all in plain, accessible language. Deliberately scoped to education only with no hacking or illegal access instructions.

Process — Design Thinking Applied

1

Empathize: Most internet users struggle to recognize phishing emails, use weak passwords, and lack cybersecurity awareness especially on public Wi-Fi.

2

Define: Users need practical, accessible cybersecurity guidance to protect themselves from common online threats.

3

Ideate: Evaluated four concepts — selected Digital Safety AI as best serving the goal of raising broad awareness and encouraging safe online behavior.

4

Prototype: Built as a Custom GPT with tailored instructions covering threats, safe tips, password practices, and responsible tech use. Excluded hacking guidance.

5

Test & Iterate: Fixed technical jargon complexity and response length issues by editing prompts for simplicity, brevity, and focus.

Artifact 02 — Workshop 2 — Discussion 2.4

Adaptive Learning in Change Leadership — A DevOps Perspective

Leadership Discussion

A leadership discussion post exploring adaptive learning in the context of organizational change — grounded in a real AWS cloud migration experience and an AI-generated scenario about a fintech multi-cloud deployment. Written for a technical leadership audience that understands what it costs when plans fail to adapt to reality in production environments.

Objective

To articulate the principles of adaptive learning in change leadership through a personal professional experience, and to use AI as a tool for generating a hypothetical scenario that illustrates those principles in a technically credible context relevant to DevOps and cloud engineering leadership.

Description

The discussion presents two complementary perspectives on adaptive learning: a real AWS cloud migration where a 60% cost overrun and CI/CD latency issues were hidden rather than addressed — demonstrating the cost of non-adaptive leadership — and an AI-generated fintech scenario where a leader's adaptive response to a payment processing latency gap saves far more than the delay cost.

Part 1 — Personal Experience: AWS Cloud Migration

During a major on-premises to AWS migration, the plan looked clean on paper. Two months in, cloud costs were running 60% over projection — unaccounted egress fees — and the dev team's CI/CD pipelines were breaking due to latency in the new environment. The non-adaptive response: protect the timeline and report green status to executives while engineering quietly firefights. The adaptive response: treat the overrun and latency as feedback signals, pause, diagnose, recalibrate the architecture, and renegotiate the timeline with transparency.

The key lesson: in DevOps, protecting the plan rather than the outcome is always a failure mode. Adaptive leadership means treating production signals as data, not as inconveniences to manage around.

Part 2 — AI-Generated Scenario (ChatGPT-4o)

AI Tool Disclosed: ChatGPT-4o

A fintech startup is migrating its entire payment processing infrastructure to a multi-cloud architecture across AWS and Azure. At day 45 of a 90-day plan, payment processing latency is 3x higher than the legacy system during peak transaction windows — a performance gap that would violate enterprise client SLA commitments. A non-adaptive leader doubles down on the timeline. An adaptive leader treats the latency data as a critical feedback signal, brings in cloud architecture specialists, discovers a database connection pooling incompatibility with the multi-cloud routing setup, and delays go-live by 30 days. The board is disappointed — until they learn that shipping on time would have triggered SLA breach penalties worth 10x the delay cost.

Concepts Demonstrated

Adaptive Learning Change Leadership AI/ML Integration Cloud Migration Feedback-Driven Decision Making Generative AI for Scenario Planning

Value Proposition

For technology leaders evaluating AI/ML integration talent, this artifact demonstrates leadership thinking — not just technical skill. The ability to recognize when a plan needs to change, make that call with data rather than ego, and communicate it transparently to stakeholders is what separates engineers who can lead from engineers who can execute. This discussion shows I understand the difference and can articulate it clearly with real professional experience behind it.

Unique Value

My DevOps background makes this analysis concrete rather than abstract. I have seen what happens when infrastructure migrations are declared on-time while actually failing — it is not a hypothetical leadership challenge, it is a production incident waiting to happen. The adaptive leadership principles I discuss here are the same ones that separate reliable infrastructure teams from ones that quietly accumulate technical debt and hide it in status reports.

Relevance

As AI/ML systems are integrated into production infrastructure, the leadership challenges become more complex — not less. AI models drift, data distributions shift, latency profiles change under load. The adaptive learning principles that apply to human change leadership apply equally to managing AI systems in production. Leaders who can update their approach based on real-time feedback will build more reliable AI infrastructure than those who execute static plans and hope the system self-corrects.
